About us

The Family Map Inventory was developed by experts on child development, home environment, child care education, mental health, and parenting. These professionals are dedicated to translating research to childcare practice.

Leanne Whiteside-Mansell, Ed.D.

is a Professor in the Department of Family and Preventive Medicine at the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences and is the developer of the Family Map Inventory. She has more than 100 presentations and publications related to parenting and child development in at-risk populations. She led two research projects to develop the Family Map. The first was a Head Start Measurement Development project that resulted in the Early Childhood Family Map. The second project was to develop and test the effectiveness of the Infant Toddler Family Map in increasing parent-teacher partnerships. She continues to lead the Family Map team in the development of additional applications of the instrument.

Lorraine McKelvey, Ph.D.

is an Associate Professor of Family & Preventive Medicine at the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences. Her background is in developmental psychology, with experience in program evaluation research, university-community partnerships and analysis of longitudinal data. Dr. McKelvey is the lead evaluator of several statewide initiatives including Arkansas’ childcare quality rating and improvement system and the state’s maternal, infant, and early childhood home visiting programs. Dr. McKelvey has most actively been involved in the use of the Family Map in home visiting programs. Her research work has demonstrated the validity of the Family Map as a valid ACEs screening tool in young children.

Robert H. Bradley, Ph.D.

is a joint professor in the T. Denny Sanford School of Social and Family Dynamics and the Department of Psychology at Arizona State University. Dr. Bradley has an extensive background in the measurement of family characteristics. He developed the HOME Inventory which is a widely used measure of the home environment. He has served on the editorial board of 10 professional journals and has been an investigator for the Early Head Start National Evaluation Study and the NICHD Study of Child Care and Youth Development. Professor Bradley’s primary areas of research include relations between experiences in the home environment and children’s development, child care, and early education.

Nicola Conners-Burrow, Ph. D.

is a Professor of Family & Preventive Medicine at the University of Arkansas for Medical Sciences. Her work focuses on children at risk of early social-emotional concerns, including those impacted by trauma, maternal substance abuse and depression. Her research has led to unique national collaborations and interventions to support young children’s mental health. With a background in educational psychology and research, she has more than 45 peer-reviewed manuscripts published or in press and has served as the principal investigator on numerous projects.

Frequently
Asked Questions

Getting the information from the Family Map is only a small part of the reason to use it. Conducting the interview allows you to connect to the family and start a real partnership – a conversation.
The Family Map Inventories were developed to be conducted by the educator that spends the most time with the child in center-based programs or the assigned visitor in home visiting programs. The interview can also be conducted by a family services specialist who then shares the information with the educational team.
The interview should be conducted with the person who spends the most time caring for the child. The child should also be at the interview. Welcome any other adults who help take care of the child.
We recommend that the Family Map interview be conducted twice a year. This allows programs to document the services they provide and gauge progress. We have worked with programs that show reductions in risks from fall to spring of a program year. We know family risks and strengths can change quickly. We have found that new risks are identified in the second interview and you’ll note some risks that have resolved.
The Family Map has most often been used at home visits after the family/child have been in the program for a few weeks. However, some programs use it at enrollment and report to us that they are happy with the result. Some programs choose to break up the interview over 2-3 short meetings with the parent starting at enrollment.
Typically, the Family Map Interview takes 30-45 minutes to complete.
Review the topics in the Family Map to identify referral options and community resources your interviewers will likely need. Review your agency policy and protocols on confidentiality and mandatory reporting. In rare cases, the Family Map interview could identify child abuse, domestic violence or mental health issues.

If you have a trauma informed classroom initiative, consider how the information from the Family Map can be shared with the educators working with the target child.
